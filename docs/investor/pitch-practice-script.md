# Pitch practice — 5 investor archetypes

> Practice with a friend who reads each archetype's brief, then asks the
> questions in order. Time-box your answer at 90 seconds per question.
> If you can't answer cleanly, that's the question to drill before the
> next real meeting.
>
> Run all 5 archetypes back-to-back at least once before any seed-stage
> meeting. Total time ~90 minutes.

## How to use this with a friend

1. Hand them this doc, ask them to pick one archetype
2. They read the archetype's brief silently
3. They ask the questions in order. Don't read ahead — the value is the
   surprise
4. Time each answer. If you exceed 90 seconds, the friend cuts you off
5. After all 6 questions, the friend gives feedback in 60 seconds: what
   was strong, what was weak, what was unclear
6. Move to the next archetype

If you can hold all 5 archetypes in one sitting without losing energy,
you're pitch-ready.

## Archetype 1 — The Skeptic Partner

**Profile:** Senior partner at a tier-1 fund (Sequoia / Lightspeed /
Accel). Has seen 200 decks this year. Tired. Defaults to no.
Pattern-matches early; if your first 60 seconds aren't sharp, they're
already drafting a polite-pass email in their head.

**What they care about:** Why this. Why now. Why you. Defensibility.
Won't fund a content business at venture multiples.

**Their tone:** dry, low-affect, asking direct questions and listening
for the founder to flinch.

### Questions in order

1. *"Walk me through the business in 30 seconds."* (Time it. Cut at 30.)

2. *"What's the lie in your deck?"*

3. *"If I gave you ₹2 Cr today and told you you can't raise again for
   36 months, do you still run this? How?"*

4. *"What's the median outcome in 5 years? Not the dream — the median."*

5. *"Why hasn't TripAdvisor or Lonely Planet eaten this?"*

6. *"What's defensible here in 24 months that someone with $5M can't
   replicate?"*

**Rubric for the listener:**
- Did the founder name a real risk unprompted? (good)
- Did the founder say "we'll figure it out" anywhere? (bad)
- Did the founder concede a weakness and reframe it as a learning?
  (excellent)

## Archetype 2 — The Technical Partner

**Profile:** Engineering-background partner at an early-stage fund.
Reads the codebase before the meeting. Pattern-matches to "is this
buildable" rather than "is this fundable" — both questions matter, but
this one starts with the code.

**What they care about:** Architecture sanity. Bus factor. Vendor risk.
Whether the founder understands what's been built. Whether the AI-
generated codebase is a liability.

**Their tone:** specific, technical, asks for screenshots and live URLs.

### Questions in order

1. *"You said the codebase is 80% Claude-Code-assisted. What's your IP
   posture? What if a snippet is GPL-contaminated?"*

2. *"Open Sentry. Show me the last 10 errors live."* (If you can't, lose
   the meeting honestly.)

3. *"Walk me through what happens technically when a beware report is
   submitted — submit-to-publish, end to end."*

4. *"What's your bus factor today and what's it post-funding?"*

5. *"You're doing AI-assisted verification. What does Claude actually do
   in that flow, and what's the false-acceptance rate?"*

6. *"Vendor concentration — Supabase + Vercel + Anthropic. What's your
   migration path if any of those becomes unaffordable?"*

**Rubric:**
- Did they cite specific file paths or table names from memory? (good)
- Did they overstate AI capability? (kills credibility)
- Did they have an honest answer to the bus factor? (must)

## Archetype 3 — The Women-Focused / Impact Investor

**Profile:** Partner at a fund with an explicit women / DEI / impact
thesis. Often more sympathetic to the mission, sometimes less rigorous
on unit economics. Risk: they might fund you for the wrong reasons,
which catches up at Series A.

**What they care about:** Real impact. Founder authenticity. Whether
the women-only restriction is performative or substantive. Trust and
safety posture. Treatment of contributors.

**Their tone:** warm, often asks personal questions, builds rapport
before getting to numbers.

### Questions in order

1. *"Why is this work yours to do? Tell me a personal story that makes
   this product inevitable from your hands."*

2. *"How do you handle trans women — what's the policy, and how was it
   built?"*

3. *"Walk me through what happens if a contributor is harassed offline
   because of something she wrote on your platform."*

4. *"When the Beware Board moderates a report against a small business
   that turns out to be mostly run by a woman of marginalized
   background, how do you weigh that?"*

5. *"What do contributors get? Take me through one contributor's
   economics — earnings, time investment, autonomy."*

6. *"What does success look like for this fund's LPs? They're patient
   but they're not infinitely patient."*

**Rubric:**
- Did the founder's personal story land authentically? (essential)
- Were the policy answers concrete (not vibes)? (must)
- Did the contributor economics math check out? (must)

## Archetype 4 — The Growth-Only Investor

**Profile:** Partner at a fund focused on growth and unit economics. Less
interested in mission, more interested in CAC / LTV / TAM. India-
consumer-focused.

**What they care about:** Funnel math. Cohort retention. CAC payback.
Channel mix. Whether this scales to ₹100 Cr ARR or caps at ₹20 Cr.

**Their tone:** spreadsheet-out, fast, may interrupt.

### Questions in order

1. *"Walk me through the funnel from organic visitor to paid member.
   Numbers, not narrative."* (Open the model on screen.)

2. *"What's your D30 retention today? Don't say 'we're early.' Show me
   any cohort."*

3. *"CAC for the Indian woman segment — what number are you assuming,
   and have you tested it with paid spend?"*

4. *"Year 3 ARR you said is ₹6–8 Cr. What's the path from there to
   ₹50 Cr? Or is this a smaller business?"*

5. *"Booking.com launches a 'solo women' filter and a 5-page guide
   tomorrow. What happens to your numbers?"*

6. *"You said ₹999/year membership. At what conversion rate does this
   business work, and what's the sensitivity?"*

**Rubric:**
- Did the founder open the actual model live? (essential)
- Could they navigate sensitivity (changing one assumption mid-question)?
  (excellent)
- Did they give a defensible LTV/CAC ratio? (must)

## Archetype 5 — The Strategic / Corporate VC

**Profile:** Partner at a corporate venture arm — MakeMyTrip, Booking
Holdings, OYO Ventures, BookingCom, an insurance carrier. Cares about
strategic value to the parent. May offer integration, distribution,
data partnership.

**What they care about:** Whether you're a tuck-in acquisition target,
a data partner, or a competitive threat. Will you exit to them? What's
the relationship architecture during the round?

**Their tone:** asks about partnerships, thinks aloud about how you'd
plug into the parent.

### Questions in order

1. *"In 5 years, who buys you? Walk me through the realistic exit set."*

2. *"What's your B2B story? The Beware Board incident dataset — can a
   corporate travel program license that for women employees?"*

3. *"Insurance angle — if a major insurer wanted to bundle Wander
   Women membership into a women's-travel-insurance product, what's
   your read on that?"*

4. *"How do you think about preferring strategic capital vs financial
   capital at this stage?"*

5. *"If we wrote you a term sheet, would you take a board observer
   seat from us?"*

6. *"Is there a non-financial value-add that would matter more to you
   than the dollars right now? Distribution? Hiring? PR?"*

**Rubric:**
- Did the founder articulate a clear acquirer set? (must)
- Did they recognize the B2B safety-data optionality? (good)
- Did they handle the strategic-vs-financial question with maturity?
  (excellent)

## After all 5 — debrief questions

The friend asks you these last:

1. Which archetype felt hardest? Why?
2. Which question landed for you? Which felt false?
3. If you could go back to one answer, which one and what would you
   change?
4. What's the single most important thing you'd practice before the
   next real meeting?

Write the answers down. They become the agenda for your next prep.

## Calibration over time

Real investors don't fit cleanly into one archetype — they're a mix.
But after running these 5 enough times, you'll start hearing real
investors in archetype-language: "Ah, this is mostly an Archetype 3
with some Archetype 4 mixed in." That recognition lets you toggle your
answer style mid-meeting, which is the most valuable pitch skill there
is.

## When you're ready

You're ready when:
- You can hit time on the 30-second walkthrough every time
- You can name a real weakness in your business unprompted
- You can switch from numbers (Archetype 4) to mission (Archetype 3)
  in 10 seconds without sounding inauthentic
- You can take a hard question and reframe it without losing ground

Run this script with one friend. Note their feedback. Run it again
with a different friend a week later. The goal isn't to perfect the
answers; it's to make the discomfort of these questions familiar.
