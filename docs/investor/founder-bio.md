> Fill this in with your real bio. Then export as a single page (PDF or
> Notion) and put it in the data room. This is the doc investors read
> after the first pitch and before the second meeting.
>
> **Critical:** I don't know your actual background. Every section
> below has placeholder text in [brackets]. Replace each one before
> using this doc.

## Why a founder one-pager

Investors at seed mostly fund founders, not products. After the first
pitch, the partner emails their associate: *"Look up the founder."*
What the associate finds — your LinkedIn + Twitter + this one-pager —
determines whether you get the next meeting.

You control this surface. Make it work for you.

## Filling each section — guidance

### One-line positioning

Should be 8–14 words. Should answer "what is this person about?"
Should NOT be a job title.

**Bad:** "Founder & CEO of Wander Women"
**Good:** "Travel writer turned founder, building verified safety
intel for women in India"

### About — paragraph 1

This is the LinkedIn-style paragraph. Concrete companies, specific
roles, named accomplishments. Investors verify this.

**Test:** read it back. Does every sentence have a specific noun
(company name, project, metric)? If not, rewrite.

### About — paragraph 2

This is the why-you paragraph. Personal but not over-personal.
Authentic but not therapy.

**Bad:** "I've always loved solo travel..."
**Good:** "After a 2018 trip to Hampi where my hostel turned out
to be unsafe, I started a private spreadsheet for women friends. It
grew to 200 women. Wander Women is the productized version of that
spreadsheet."

### Background

Strict reverse-chronological. Don't pad. If your background is short
— 2-3 roles is fine. Empty space says "this person doesn't add
fluff."

### Why this product (3 bullets)

Each bullet should be one sentence and *specific*. Avoid:

- "Passion for women's safety" (vague)
- "I want to make the world better" (vague)
- "Travel is broken" (clichéd)

Prefer:

- "I noticed Indian women I knew were using WhatsApp groups to
  share scam warnings — privately, slowly, and only with friends.
  The information existed; the platform didn't."
- "DPDP Act 2023 makes anonymous UGC platforms legally riskier; we
  built ours verification-first from day one."
- "I've been the primary maintainer of a private safety doc for
  200+ women travelers since 2019. The user is me."

### Travel credentials

Concrete, dated, specific. Investors verify this against your social
media. If you said "5 solo trips to Goa," they will check Instagram.

### What I'm not

This is the most-skipped section by founders, and the most-noticed
by investors. List 1–3 honest gaps. Each gap gets paired with what
you're doing about it.

**Why this works:** founders who name gaps in writing are perceived
as more self-aware AND more truthful. Investors who already noticed
the gap (they will) will mentally dock you for not naming it. Naming
it pre-empts the dock.

### Advisors

Only include real advisors. Investors WILL email them and ask. If
"advisor X" doesn't know they're your advisor, you've blown the
relationship and the round.

If you don't have advisors yet, leave the section out. Or replace
with "Currently in conversation with [N] potential advisors; will
update."

### Recent writing / press

Concrete links, not vague claims. If you've never been published,
skip the section.

## Distribution format

### As a PDF
1. Paste the filled-in template into a single Google Doc
2. Format with the same fonts as the product (Cormorant for the
   name, DM Mono for body)
3. Export as PDF, file as `[founder-name]-bio.pdf`
4. Upload to data room

### As a Notion page
Same content, Notion-formatted. Public link.

### As a LinkedIn About section
Use the "About" two paragraphs as the basis for your LinkedIn
About — they should match. Different audiences, same story.

### As Twitter bio
The one-line positioning becomes your Twitter bio. Keep them
identical for consistency.

## What investors do with this doc

In order of probability:

1. **Glance during the first call** to verify they remember who
   you are
2. **Forward to their associate** for "look up the founder" tasks
3. **Reference in partner meetings** when explaining who you are
4. **Quote from in their internal write-up** if they decide to fund

Optimize for #4. The associate's write-up is what actually moves
the deal. Make their job easy.

## Updating

Refresh:
- After any new advisor signs on
- After every major press piece
- After every quarter (refresh the dates / numbers)
- Before every meeting where you'll send the doc

Don't refresh:
- For minor edits — minor edits look anxious
- For job-title nuance — the one-line positioning is more important

## What this doc is NOT

- A resume — too dry
- A LinkedIn dump — too long
- A vision statement — wrong audience
- A sob story — investors don't fund pity

It's a *focused* portrait that helps the investor make a yes-or-no
decision about you specifically.
